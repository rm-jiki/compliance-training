🏛️ What is MACC?

Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (SPRM)

MACC (Suruhanjaya Pencegahan Rasuah Malaysia) is Malaysia's primary anti-corruption authority, established in 2009 under the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission Act 2009.

  • Eradicate corruption in public and private sectors
  • Eradicate abuse of power by officials and employees
  • Eradicate malpractice in business and government dealings

Bribe

A type of corruption. It refers to the act of corruptly:

  • Authorising a payment or benefit
  • Giving something of value
  • Receiving any gratification

Corruption

The act of giving or receiving any gratification or reward in the form of cash or in-kind of high value for performing a task in relation to one's job.

💰 Types of Bribe

Bribes Come in Many Forms

A bribe is not always cash. Be aware of all the ways gratification can be offered or received:

  • Cash — Direct monetary payments
  • Gifts & Entertainment — Vouchers, hampers, meals, events, luxury items
  • Travel & Events — Paid trips, cruises, concert tickets, sponsored holidays
  • Charity / Political Donations — Made on your behalf to conceal the bribe
  • Preferential Selection — Awarding a job or contract to a family member or associated vendor in exchange for personal gain
🎭 Scenario — What would you do?

During the festive season, a supplier sends your home address a hamper worth RM800 with a card saying "Thank you for your continued support — looking forward to renewing our contract next quarter." What should you do?

✅ Correct! A high-value gift received just before a contract renewal is a red flag. Under SuperApp Group's GHE policy, all gifts must be declared. If it exceeds the acceptable threshold or could influence a business decision, it must be returned and reported to your compliance officer.
❌ Incorrect. Gifts timed around business decisions — especially of high value — fall under the definition of gratification. Even if your intentions are clean, accepting without declaring breaches Group policy and could expose you to MACC investigation. Always declare.

⚖️ MACC Act 2009

Four Key Offences You Must Know

  • Section 16 — Soliciting or Receiving Gratification (Bribe)
  • Section 17(A) — Offering or Giving Gratification (Bribe)
  • Section 18 — Intending to Deceive (False Claim)
  • Section 23 — Using Office or Position for Gratification / Abuse of Power
📰 Real Cases — Sections 16 & 17A
⚠️ Case Study — Section 16: Soliciting / Receiving a Bribe

Rosmah Mansor — RM1.25 Billion Sarawak Solar Project

Rosmah Mansor was found guilty of all three counts of corruption related to the RM1.25 billion Sarawak rural schools' solar energy project.

She had solicited RM187.5 million from a company managing director, through her former aide, as an inducement to help the company secure the contract. She was also found guilty of receiving RM5 million and RM1.5 million in cash bribes.

Verdict

Sentenced to 10 years in prison for each of the three charges (concurrent). Fined RM970 million — or an additional 10 years in prison if unpaid.

📰 Real Cases — Section 18
⚠️ Case Study — Section 18: False Claims

RM15 Million Fake Claims — Home Ministry C4i Contract

Six individuals including a company director, a CFO, and two police officers were arrested for submitting false claims totalling RM15 million between 2022 and 2025, related to a maintenance contract for the Command & Control, Communication & Computer Integration (C4i) system.

MACC raided 15 locations across Johor, Selangor, Putrajaya, and Kuala Lumpur. Four luxury vehicles worth RM1.3 million were seized, along with 51 frozen bank accounts and assets estimated at RM3.5 million.

Investigation Status

Case investigated under Section 18 of MACC Act 2009 and Section 4(1) of the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA).

📰 Real Cases — Section 23
⚠️ Case Study — Section 23: Abuse of Position

Health Officer Swings Contracts to Own Company

An Assistant Environmental Health Officer in Papar was convicted on 20 counts of using his position to direct supply contracts — food and beverage, minor repairs, office supplies — to his own company, Riched Enterprise, obtaining bribes totalling RM30,793.

Verdict

20 years total jail (1 year per charge, concurrent). Fined a total of RM240,670. The lesson: no amount is too small to attract prosecution.

🎭 Scenario — What would you do?

You are in the procurement team and your manager asks you to raise an invoice for RM50,000 to a vendor for "consulting services rendered" — but you know no such services were provided. Your manager assures you it has been "approved from the top." What do you do?

✅ Correct! Raising a false invoice is a criminal offence under Section 18 of the MACC Act — regardless of who approved it. "Following orders" is not a legal defence. Refuse, document everything, and report through the confidential ethics channel. You are protected as a whistleblower.
❌ Incorrect. Participating in a false claim — even at a manager's request — makes you personally liable under Section 18 of MACC Act 2009. Senior approval does not protect you. You must refuse and report it confidentially. The law protects whistleblowers.

💸 Fines & Penalties
Individual Penalty
MACC Act 2009 — Ss. 16, 17, 18 & 23
  • Fine of at least 5× the bribe amount or RM10,000 — whichever is higher
  • Jail up to 20 years per offence
Corporate Penalty
MACC Amendment Act 2018 — S. 17A
  • Fine of at least 10× the bribe amount or RM1 million — whichever is higher
  • Jail up to 20 years per offence
SuperApp Consequence
Group Policy
  • Immediate dismissal
  • Barred from future employment in the Group
  • Reported to MACC and relevant authorities
📋 What Our ABC Policy Covers

Gifts, Hospitality & Entertainment (GHE)

CONDITIONAL
  • GHE should never be requested or provided to influence a decision
  • Staff and Directors may accept "appropriate" gifts to maintain good relationships
  • Must be declared in the Gift Register

Facilitation Payments

STRICTLY NO
  • Defined as small bribes to speed up a business decision, shipment, or transaction
  • Strictly prohibited under Group policy — no exceptions
  • Report any requests immediately

Donation, Sponsorship & Charity

WITH APPROVAL
  • Must seek approval and fill in the Declaration Form (Appendix 1)
  • Strictly not to gain or retain any business
  • Purpose must be declared and documented
🤝 Engaging with Third Parties

Your Responsibilities With Vendors & Partners

The Group can be held liable for bribery and corruption committed by third parties acting on its behalf. Every employee has a duty to:

  • Conduct due diligence on all external parties the Group contracts with or hires
  • Ensure all payments made to third parties are for proper and legitimate reimbursements
  • The Group will not enter or continue a relationship with any third party who cannot comply with Group policy
  • If a third party raises red flags, escalate immediately to the Risk & Compliance Department
🎭 Scenario — What would you do?

Your team is onboarding a new logistics vendor. During due diligence, you discover the vendor has no registered business address, issues vague invoices with no description of services, and the director is the spouse of a government official who approves your company's operating licences. What do you do?

✅ Correct! Multiple red flags here — vague invoicing, no registered address, and a conflict of interest with a government official. Under Group policy, you must not proceed with a third party who raises compliance concerns. Escalate to Risk & Compliance for a full assessment. Urgency is never a reason to bypass due diligence.
❌ Incorrect. These are serious red flags that must be escalated — not worked around. The Group can be held liable under MACC Section 17A for corruption committed through third parties. Proceeding without clearance puts you and the company at serious legal risk.

📣 Your Duty to Speak Up

Silence Makes You Complicit

Every employee — regardless of seniority — has a personal obligation to report suspected bribery or corruption. You are legally protected when you report in good faith.

  • Report to your direct manager or HR if you feel safe doing so
  • Use the anonymous Ethics Hotline if you prefer confidentiality
  • Email the Compliance Officer directly via People Connect
  • Document everything — dates, names, what was said or offered
  • Do not investigate alone — hand it to the compliance team
  • Retaliation against whistleblowers is a serious disciplinary offence

🎭 Final Assessment — What would you do?

A colleague confides in you that they gave a government officer RM500 "under the table" to speed up a licence approval last month. They ask you to keep it secret. What is your obligation?

✅ Excellent! You have a duty to report known or suspected corruption — even when it involves a colleague or has already happened. This is a facilitation payment under MACC Section 16/17A. Report it through the confidential channel. You are legally protected for reporting in good faith. The Group's whistleblower policy ensures you will not face retaliation.
❌ Incorrect. Staying silent about a known corrupt act can make you an accessory. Both facilitation payments (Section 16/17A) and covering them up can attract criminal liability. You must report it confidentially. The ethics hotline protects your identity and you cannot be penalised for reporting in good faith.
