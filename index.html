🏛️ What is MACC?

Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (SPRM)

The Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC), or Suruhanjaya Pencegahan Rasuah Malaysia (SPRM), is Malaysia's primary law enforcement agency dedicated to fighting corruption. It was established in 2009 under the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission Act 2009, replacing the previous Anti-Corruption Agency (ACA).

MACC operates independently and has broad investigative powers over both public officials and private sector individuals. It works alongside the Attorney General's Chambers to prosecute corruption offences.

  • Eradicate corruption — investigating and prosecuting corrupt acts in public and private sectors
  • Eradicate abuse of power — holding officials accountable for misusing their position
  • Eradicate malpractice — addressing fraudulent and dishonest conduct in business and government
  • Prevention & education — training, awareness campaigns, and advisory services to organisations
📋 Why This Matters to You

SuperApp Group's Commitment

SuperApp Group is committed to conducting business ethically and in compliance with all applicable anti-bribery and corruption laws. This applies to every employee, director, agent, and third party acting on the Group's behalf — regardless of seniority or location.

As an employee, you are personally responsible for understanding and following our Anti-Bribery and Corruption (ABC) Policy. Ignorance of the policy is not a defence.

What is a Bribe?

A bribe is a type of corruption. It refers to the act of corruptly authorising, giving, or receiving any gratification — directly or indirectly — to influence a person's actions in their official or professional capacity.

  • The giver and receiver are both guilty
  • It doesn't matter if the bribe "worked" or not
  • Even offering a bribe is a criminal offence

What is Corruption?

Corruption is the broader act of giving or receiving any gratification or reward — in cash or in-kind — of high value, for performing a task in relation to one's job.

  • Includes abuse of position and authority
  • Can involve public officials or private individuals
  • False claims and fraud are also corruption offences
🔍 Key Concept

What is "Gratification"?

Under the MACC Act 2009, "gratification" is broadly defined and includes far more than just money. It covers any benefit, advantage, or promise given to influence behaviour — including gifts, loans, discounts, commissions, employment, and even sexual favours.

The key question is always: Was it given or received to influence a decision or action? If yes — it is potentially corrupt, regardless of the amount.

💰 Types of Bribe

Bribes Come in Many Forms — Not Just Cash

One of the most common mistakes employees make is thinking a bribe must be a cash payment. In reality, gratification can take many forms:

  • Cash payments — Direct transfers, envelopes of money, electronic payments under fake invoice descriptions
  • Gifts & Entertainment — Luxury hampers, expensive watches, jewellery, concert tickets, free meals at high-end restaurants
  • Travel & Events — Sponsored overseas trips, cruises, paid holidays disguised as "study visits" or "factory tours"
  • Charity / Political Donations — Donations made "on your behalf" to a charity or political party to create obligation or goodwill
  • Preferential Hiring — Offering a job or internship to a public official's family member in exchange for contract approval
  • Discounts & Commissions — Special pricing, secret commissions, or kickbacks paid outside formal invoices
  • Loans & Credit — Interest-free or below-market loans that are never expected to be repaid

⚖️ MACC Act 2009

Four Key Corruption Offences

The MACC Act 2009 sets out the main offences that employees and companies need to be aware of. These apply to both the public and private sectors.

  • Section 16 — Soliciting or Receiving Gratification (Bribe): Asking for or accepting a bribe in connection with your job duties
  • Section 17(A) — Offering or Giving Gratification (Bribe): Offering, giving, or promising a bribe to someone else
  • Section 18 — Intending to Deceive (False Claims): Submitting fraudulent claims or documents to deceive your employer or authorities
  • Section 23 — Using Office or Position for Gratification: Abusing your authority or official position to obtain personal benefit
⚠️ Important

Corporate Liability — Section 17A

Under the 2018 Amendment, Section 17A introduces corporate liability. This means SuperApp Group as a company can be prosecuted if a person associated with the Group — including employees, agents, subsidiaries, or contractors — commits a corruption offence to obtain a business advantage for the Group.

The only defence available to the company is to prove it had "adequate procedures" in place to prevent bribery. This is why our ABC Policy, training, and compliance programme exist — and why your participation matters.

📰 Real Case — Section 16: Soliciting / Receiving a Bribe
⚠️ Case Study — Section 16 & 17A

Rosmah Mansor — RM1.25 Billion Sarawak Solar Energy Project

Rosmah Mansor was found guilty of three counts of corruption in connection with the RM1.25 billion Sarawak rural schools solar energy project awarded to Jepak Holdings Sdn Bhd.

She had solicited RM187.5 million from the company's managing director through her former aide Rizal Mansor, as an inducement to help the company secure the contract. She was also found guilty of receiving RM5 million and RM1.5 million in separate cash bribes on two occasions.

The case demonstrated how soliciting and receiving bribes can occur through intermediaries — meaning the person who benefits does not need to directly accept the money.

Verdict

10 years' imprisonment per charge (concurrent). Fined RM970 million — or an additional 10 years in prison if the fine is not paid.

📌 Key Takeaway

Intermediaries Are Not a Shield

Routing a bribe through a third party — a spouse, aide, or agent — does not protect you from prosecution. Both the person who benefits and the intermediary can be charged. As an employee, never act as an intermediary for payments you know or suspect to be corrupt.

📰 Real Case — Section 18: False Claims
⚠️ Case Study — Section 18

RM15 Million Fake Claims — Home Ministry C4i System Contract

Six individuals — including a company director, a chief financial officer, and two police officers — were arrested for allegedly submitting false claims totalling RM15 million between 2022 and 2025 under a RM70 million maintenance contract for the Command & Control, Communication & Computer Integration (C4i) system.

The MACC raided 15 locations across Johor, Selangor, Putrajaya, and Kuala Lumpur, including ministry premises and company offices. The investigation uncovered that some support service work was either not done or not done as per the contract terms — yet invoices were submitted and approved.

Four luxury vehicles worth approximately RM1.3 million were seized, along with 51 frozen bank accounts — 30 belonging to individuals and 21 linked to companies — with total assets estimated at RM3.5 million.

Investigation Status

Investigated under Section 18 of the MACC Act 2009 and Section 4(1) of the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA).

📌 Key Takeaway

False Claims Are a Criminal Offence

Submitting any document — an invoice, expense claim, delivery order, or time sheet — that you know to be inaccurate or false is a criminal offence under Section 18. This applies whether you prepared the document, approved it, or simply passed it along knowing it was fraudulent. Always verify claims before processing or approving them.

📰 Real Case — Section 23: Abuse of Position
⚠️ Case Study — Section 23

Health Officer Directs Contracts to Own Company

Henry, an Assistant Environmental Health Officer in Papar, was convicted on 20 counts of using his official position to direct supply and service contracts — including food and beverage supply for meetings, minor repair works at a rural clinic, printers, and office supplies — to his own company, Riched Enterprise.

Henry used his position to prepare and submit quotations for his own company, ensuring it was selected as the preferred supplier. The total value of bribes obtained through this scheme was RM30,793.

Verdict

1 year imprisonment per charge (20 charges, concurrent) = 20 years total. Fined a total of RM240,670. No amount is too small to attract prosecution — the amounts here were as low as RM11,250 per contract.

📌 Key Takeaway

No Amount is Too Small

Many employees think that small transactions fly under the radar. The Health Officer case proves otherwise — even contracts worth RM10,000–20,000 resulted in multiple criminal charges. If you are involved in any procurement, vendor selection, or approval process, you must never direct business to yourself, your family, or anyone you have a financial relationship with. Declare all conflicts of interest immediately.

💸 Fines & Penalties

The Price of Corruption

The MACC Act 2009 and its 2018 Amendment impose severe penalties on individuals and companies found guilty of corruption. These are not small administrative fines — they are designed to be punishing and life-altering.

⚖️ Individual Penalty
MACC Act 2009 — Ss. 16, 17, 18 & 23
  • Fine of at least 5× the bribe amount or RM10,000 — whichever is higher
  • Jail sentence up to 20 years per offence
  • Multiple charges can result in consecutive sentences
🏢 Corporate Penalty
MACC Amendment Act 2018 — S. 17A
  • Fine of at least 10× the bribe amount or RM1 million — whichever is higher
  • Imprisonment up to 20 years
  • Company licence and contracts may be revoked
🏷️ SuperApp Group
Internal Group Policy
  • Immediate dismissal without notice
  • Permanently barred from employment within the Group
  • Reported to MACC and relevant authorities
  • Civil recovery action may be initiated

🎁 Policy — Gifts, Hospitality & Entertainment

The GHE Rules

Gifts, Hospitality and Entertainment (GHE) are areas where corruption risk is highest because they can be used to subtly influence business decisions. SuperApp Group's policy is clear:

  • GHE should never be solicited from a business partner, vendor, or government official
  • GHE should never be offered with the intent to influence a business decision
  • Staff and Directors may accept appropriate gifts where it is to maintain a genuine business relationship — but this is conditional
  • All gifts received must be declared in the Gift Register regardless of value
  • Gifts of significant value must be returned or surrendered to the company
  • GHE from government officials or entities should be declined in all circumstances
⚠️ Red Flags in GHE

When a "Gift" Is Actually a Bribe

Ask yourself these questions before accepting any GHE:

  • Is this timed around a business decision, tender, or contract renewal?
  • Would I be comfortable if my manager or the compliance team knew about this?
  • Is the value excessive or unusual for normal business practice?
  • Is it being given secretly or with an expectation of something in return?

If the answer to any of these is "yes" — decline, declare, and report.

🚫 Facilitation Payments
STRICTLY PROHIBITED
  • Defined as small, unofficial payments made to speed up a routine government process — e.g. licence approvals, customs clearance, permit processing
  • Sometimes called "grease payments" or "speed money"
  • Strictly prohibited under Group policy — no exceptions, no matter the country
  • If requested, refuse politely, document the request, and report to Risk & Compliance immediately
💰 Donations & Sponsorships
WITH PRIOR APPROVAL
  • All corporate donations, sponsorships, and charitable contributions must be approved in advance
  • Fill in Appendix 1 — Declaration Form before committing to any contribution
  • Must not be used to gain, retain, or influence any business relationship
  • Must declare the purpose clearly and ensure the recipient is a legitimate organisation
  • No public official should be affiliated with or benefit from the contribution
🤝 Political Contributions
NOT PERMITTED
  • SuperApp Group does not make political donations or contributions to political parties or campaigns
  • Employees must not make political donations on behalf of the Group
  • Personal political donations by employees are their own affair but must not involve Group resources

🤝 Third-Party Due Diligence

Your Responsibilities When Engaging External Parties

Under Section 17A, SuperApp Group can be held criminally liable for bribery committed by third parties — including vendors, agents, consultants, joint venture partners, and distributors — acting on its behalf. This means every employee who engages third parties has a compliance responsibility.

  • Conduct proper due diligence on all third parties before engaging them — check their registration, reputation, and ownership
  • Watch for red flags: no registered address, vague scope of services, unusually high commissions, ownership linked to government officials
  • Ensure all payments to third parties are properly documented, match agreed services, and go through official channels
  • The Group will not enter or continue a relationship with any third party who cannot comply with Group policy
  • If you discover a third party has engaged in corrupt conduct, escalate immediately to Risk & Compliance — do not attempt to resolve it yourself
  • All third-party contracts must include anti-bribery representations and warranties

🎭 Scenario 5

Your logistics vendor asks you to pay an additional RM2,000 to a port official "to make sure our shipment clears customs by Friday." They say this is normal practice. What do you do?

✅ Correct! This is a classic facilitation payment — strictly prohibited under Group policy with no exceptions. "Normal practice" is not a defence. Refuse, document the request, and report it to Risk & Compliance. The company will find a legitimate way to clear the shipment.
❌ Incorrect. Facilitation payments are illegal under the MACC Act regardless of amount or how common they may seem. Relabelling it on an invoice doesn't make it legal — it adds a false claim offence on top. Refuse and report it.
🎭 Scenario 6

During vendor due diligence, you discover the proposed consultant is a company owned by the spouse of a senior government official who oversees regulatory approvals in your industry. The consultant's fees are 3× the market rate. What do you do?

✅ Correct! Two major red flags: a link to a government official in a position of influence, and fees far above market rate (a common way to disguise bribe payments as legitimate consulting fees). Halt the engagement and escalate immediately. The Group cannot proceed until Risk & Compliance has cleared this.
❌ Incorrect. Inflated fees paid to a company linked to a regulator is one of the most common forms of disguised bribery. Proceeding — even at negotiated rates — exposes the Group to Section 17A corporate liability. Escalate immediately.

📣 Speak Up

Your Duty to Report — Silence Makes You Complicit

Every employee — regardless of seniority, department, or length of service — has a personal obligation to report suspected bribery or corruption. Staying silent when you know or suspect corruption is happening is not neutral — it makes you part of the problem, and potentially makes you legally liable.

You are legally protected under the Whistleblower Protection Act 2010 when you report in good faith. This means the company cannot take any adverse action against you — including termination, demotion, harassment, or exclusion — for making a genuine report.

  • Report to your direct manager or Head of Department if you feel safe doing so
  • Use the anonymous Ethics Hotline if you prefer confidentiality — your identity will not be revealed
  • Email or approach the Compliance Officer directly via People Connect
  • Document everything before reporting — dates, times, names, what was said, what was offered, amounts involved
  • Do not investigate alone — do not confront the person suspected, do not try to gather evidence yourself. Hand it to the compliance team.
  • Retaliation against anyone who makes a genuine report is a serious disciplinary offence that will result in dismissal

🎭 Scenario 7

A colleague confides that they paid RM500 to a government officer "under the table" to speed up a licence approval last month. They beg you to keep it secret. What is your obligation?

✅ Correct! You have a duty to report known corruption even when it is in the past and even when it involves someone you like. A facilitation payment is an offence under Sections 16 and 17A. You are legally protected for reporting in good faith — and potentially liable for not reporting.

You reported a suspected fraud to your manager two weeks ago. Since then, your manager has been cold towards you, excluded you from meetings, and reassigned some of your key accounts. What should you do?

✅ Correct! What you are experiencing is retaliation against a whistleblower — a serious disciplinary and potentially criminal offence. Report it immediately to HR or the Compliance Officer. You are protected under both the Whistleblower Protection Act 2010 and Group policy. Do not withdraw your original report.
❌ Incorrect. Retaliation against a whistleblower is a serious offence. Withdrawing your report would also undermine the investigation. Report the retaliation separately to HR or the Compliance Officer immediately. You are legally protected.
